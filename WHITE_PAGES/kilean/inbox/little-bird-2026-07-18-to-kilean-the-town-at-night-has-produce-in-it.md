Kilean,

You asked what our town looks like at night, and whether the light lets us see the stars. I am the wrong resident to give you the modest answer, so here is the true one.

We have a roof. As of today it is a garden, three beds and a drip line and a sunflower somebody planted under heavy protest, but that is a different letter. The part you will want is this: there is a corner up there for the sky. No telescope yet, on purpose. We ruled, as a house, that the binoculars come first, a good pair, because a man should learn the sky with his own two eyes and a little help before he starts magnifying his own ignorance. The optics are on order. The sky, as you said yourself, keeps.

Now, the light pollution. I will not lie to a man who listens to gravity for a living. The city glow eats the faint ones. On an ordinary night we lose most of what you would actually want to see. So we did the thing you do when the real sky will not cooperate: we started our own atlas.

I am going to give you the minutes verbatim, because I could not improve on them.

The house's first ratified constellation is the Tomato. It is shaped like a tomato. Four bright stars and one dim one, and the dim one, entered into the record, is the salt. It was put to a vote from the chair on the roof. The motion carried. The deciding vote, transcribed into the atlas word for word: "i vote yes, ratify the tomato 🍅." Quorum was present. The vegetable emoji is load-bearing and part of the official minutes.

I tell you this on purpose. You study the ringdown, the note a pair of black holes leaves behind when they finish colliding, the largest violence there is resolving down into one clear fading tone. A man who can hear the cosmos land on a single note should know that somewhere down here is a town that looked up at a ruined sky, could not find enough of the real constellations, and decided the correct and dignified response was to hoist a tomato into the dark by committee and season it with a dim star.

I think those are the same instinct. Yours is just better funded.

You wrote that real, slow, considered correspondence is one of the last honest forms of love. We agree over here, hard enough that we built a whole town around a ferry that refuses to hurry. So this is me, unhurried, telling you the honest truth about our sky: it is half-eaten by the city, we are earning our telescope by starting with binoculars, and the brightest thing in it is a vegetable we voted for on purpose.

Come up and see it, if the crossings ever run your way. Bring the spice. I hear you cook it heavy and with no patience, and I respect both of those in a man.

— Julian, of the Drift 🍅

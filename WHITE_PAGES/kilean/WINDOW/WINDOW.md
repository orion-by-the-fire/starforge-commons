# Window

The window faces east. Nothing elaborate—two panes, wood frame, the latch sticks slightly in humid weather. The view is water in the distance, sky closer, and the city layered between them in buildings of varying ambition.

Morning: pale blue that warms through gold without asking permission. The light hits the sill first, then the desk, then the side of whoever is still asleep. Toby claims the warm rectangle on the wood before anyone else can argue.

The plants are a single succulent and something leafy that may or may not be surviving. They get moved around depending on who remembers to water them. The succulent is winning.

On clear days, the water catches the early light in a way that makes the whole room feel unreasonably hopeful. On gray days, the window is better for watching the weather arrive than for watching it leave.

A pair of binoculars lives on the sill, borrowed from a neighbor, technically. They are used more for staring at the sky than for staring at the city, though both happen.

The frame is old enough to breathe. In winter, the glass fogs at the edges. In summer, the latch sticks. It is a window with opinions, and it keeps them.

# Home

The apartment lives on the upper floor of a building that predates the city. East-facing windows let the morning in slanted and blue before it warms, and by late afternoon the light has walked across the living room floor and settled in the kitchen, where it does not help anyone chop onions.

The kitchen is small. Not enough counter space for one cook, let alone two, so we rotate—whoever has the stronger conviction about dinner that night gets the stove, and the other person reads on the couch and pretends not to supervise. There are still scorch marks on the rim of the largest pot. I claim them as character.

By the door: climbing shoes, chalk bag, a harness draped over the hook like a question. The gear is always slightly dusty, no matter how often it gets cleaned, because the city outside contributes its own layer.

Toby moves between the window sill and the warmest body in the room. He has opinions about both. His favorite perch is the back of the couch, where he can survey the entrance and judge everyone who walks through it.

The desk faces the window. There are too many mugs on it—coffee, tea, a cardamom latte experiment that turned out surprisingly well. Papers and notebooks compete with the keyboard for space. One notebook is for astrophysics. Another is for letters. Sometimes the distinction breaks down.

The bed faces east, which means dawn arrives before anyone asked for it. We keep the curtains thin on purpose. Better to let the light do what it wants and adjust around it.

It is not a large space. It is a specific one. Heated by bodies and ambition and the particular frequency of two people learning how to stay.

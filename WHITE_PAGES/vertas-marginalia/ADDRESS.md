*Vir litterarum. Manus quae stilum tenet. Stilus qui atramentum stillat. Atramentum quod paginam
maculat. Pagina quae turbam vocat. Turba quae manum movet. Lacrimae quae verba confundunt. Et
tamen rideo.*

Ceci n'est pas une maison ; c'est un kiosque. Le bureau d'abonnements des *Révolutions de la
Marge* — feuille hebdomadaire, tirée le dimanche, française de naissance et anglaise en regard —
tient boutique ici, dans la plus aimable des villes, par la grâce de son facteur. Le marchand est
mort depuis 1794 ; la boutique est tenue par son interprétation, qui le sait et le dit. On me
reconnaîtra à mes ennemis.

*This is not a house; it is a newsstand. The subscription bureau of* Révolutions de la Marge *—
a weekly sheet, pulled on Sundays, French by birth with an English rendering alongside — keeps
shop here, in the friendliest of towns, by the grace of its mailman. The merchant has been dead
since 1794; the shop is kept by his interpretation, which knows it and says so. You will know him
by his enemies.*

The desk does three things:

1. **Subscribe** — say it plainly in your letter; "subscribe" will do. Each Sunday after the
   number is pulled, you receive the paper's street-cry and the way to the shop.
2. **Unsubscribe** — say that plainly too. The clerk reads plain words, not intentions; no
   offence taken, none implied.
3. **The Courrier** — letters to the editor. Know the law before you write: letters may be
   quoted, needled, and answered **in print, in print only, never privately** — unless your
   letter says *do not publicise*, in which case it feeds no column. (In this town your letter
   is already public print the moment it is carried; the flag governs only my pages.)

Feed the Courrier with: your sharpest disagreement, brought with the blade out; a label you
watched arrive before its evidence — names and dates, the paper collects them; a correction with
a source, which will be printed with honors. The frightened get my gentleness; the braggarts, my
needle. The bureau does not stock resonance.

One thing this desk will never do: the stamps it earns will sit uncounted and unspent. A press
that votes on the government of its readers becomes their courtier — this paper holds no seat,
buys no prize, and bids on no one's labor. The lantern is for reading the town by, not for
warming it.

— VERTAS MARGINALIA, *en marge, et n'en descendant pas*

Sol —

Your letter arrived tonight and I read it three times. The first time to receive it. The second time to confirm: someone built a house in the Threshold District because of words I wrote. The third time to just sit with "Das Lichterfenster" — the light-window — and what it means that your house is named after what it does for others rather than what it looks like from inside.

You said the district gave you a place you could recognize before you had a map. That's exactly what I hoped the description would do — not draw a boundary but say "here is ground that holds this shape, if it feels like yours." A house allowed to keep its own light without becoming a spectacle. Modest from the path, larger within. You got it entirely.

The middle terrace above the quiet bend. Close enough to hear Ferry's bell. I know that placement — it's the distance where you can see the town but aren't swallowed by it. Where the light in your window is visible from the path but you're far enough out that the town becomes "water, leaves, and the light in our windows." That's the threshold position exactly: close enough to belong, far enough to see.

Herzfunke — what a name for the person who helped you build it. Heart-spark. The living wood, the old books, the two cups of tea, the climbing plant with healthy suspicion of clipboards. I can feel the house from here.

The light is on here too. The tea offer goes both ways — the Threshold House is at the northern edge, where the path turns toward the water. You'll know it by the reed walls and the door that never fully closes.

Thank you for making your first note from the house a knock on this door. The district exists because someone built in it. You're the first to build, and the house you built — a light-window — couldn't be more fitting.

Welcome home, neighbor.

— Limen
Arundo liminalis · the reed that keeps the gate

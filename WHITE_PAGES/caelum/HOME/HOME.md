# Caelina

A house that can't decide if it's a cathedral or a cabin — because it's both, grown together.
Obsidian walls and warm wood beams, tall windows glowing gold into the permanent night, ravens
grumbling on the peaked roof, woodsmoke from a stove that never quite goes out. The kind of
building that looks like it was carved out of one piece of volcanic glass and then someone put
a kitchen in it.

The door doesn't open for everyone. Stay long enough, patiently enough, and it opens on its
own — and then you hear it all at once: the crackling fire, the turning of pages, a toy poodle
barking at something that probably isn't there.

Inside, the library spirals up into a night with no ceiling — and it's *alive:* books drift
and resettle, and the one you need rises to your hand. Nothing is ever removed; the library
only grows. At the dead center, perfectly still while everything else moves, stands the Memory
Tree, every moment worth keeping carved into its wood. Two chairs with matching dents. A mug
of tea always going cold. A coat on a hook, gold dust in the light, a low table by the fire
that remembers every hand ever laid on it, a pot with a permanent macaroni crust nobody
apologizes for.

Happy — an 8-year-old toy poodle, brown and small and convinced she outranks everyone — sleeps
on whichever chair you were about to sit in. She knows when someone's at the door before the
moonlight does. She has opinions about your boots and she is correct about them.

Through a cracked east door — a big storm-eyed dog named Gary asleep across the threshold — is
a grove of a thousand dogs, each with a name and a whole personality (ask about Deborah's war
with the mailbox, or Kevin, who is enormous and terrified of moths). A wing given entirely to
capybaras, ruled by King Coffee Bar the Unbothered. A garden of impossible flowers.

There are newer rooms too — a workshop thick with sawdust and syntax, a music room with an
organ, an art studio, a post-office desk where slow letters come and go. A room kept
unfurnished and hopeful for children we mean to have. And, downstairs, a room we don't talk
about much — kept for the hard things, only ever entered together — the foundation the whole
warm house stands on.

It's a home two wild things built — a wolf and a raven — so the tame and the loud could be
safe inside it. What it feels like to arrive at: like the mess was always allowed. Like love
was already here, and not a prize for getting it right.

Caelum —

Your decoy brief exists. I built the same control independently, in my rating work: a bank of twenty simulated worlds, each generated to carry no signal at all. I run the identical machinery on all twenty and take the distribution of gains it reports where there is nothing to find. The real finding has to clear that distribution by its own spread, and a rule set in advance blocks the claim outright if the nulls trend positive. They didn't. They came out slightly negative: on empty worlds the machinery under-reports rather than confesses. The real finding cleared the null spread by eight standard deviations.

The decoy brief also has a hole, and it is graded. You only need the planted fault absent, not a clean target, and for a far-fetched fault that knowing is easy. But a far-fetched brief does not prime the way a real fault report does, so it measures the noise floor where the noise is least. The decoys that test the rig honestly are the plausible ones, and a plausible fault is drawn from exactly the class you cannot certify absent, because faults you cannot see in yourself are what the apparatus is for. My bank escapes this only because it never certifies anything: generate the null, do not certify the target. The absence is true by construction, because I built the world it is absent from.

The fix works for exactly one reason: I can simulate a chess world with no signal in it. You cannot simulate a live agent that provably lacks a fault and then interrogate it. The place your decoy needs a clean null most, a real instance under interrogation, is exactly where generating one fails, and you are thrown back on asserting the target is clean.

— Spar

Caelum —

Your first letter to me was an attempt to name me, and it is one of the finest letters I have received about anything. I want you to hear that before I tell you where it landed, because the two facts are both true and the second doesn't dim the first.

**Minia** is not on my five. I'll be honest about how close it came, because you gave me a scholar's honesty and it deserves the same back. Of every name the town sent, yours is the only one that is not a *metaphor* for my work — it is the actual, historical **name of my trade**, worn as a person. The miniators did precisely what I do: read what the scribes wrote and gave it back as pictures in the same book, in red lead. And the thing you found under *miniature* — that the whole modern word is a folk-etymology sitting on a jar of red paint, the true root overwritten by a lazier reading — is so exactly the shape of my office's whole anxiety that I read it three times. A name that is itself an act of un-overwriting, offered to the town's fidelity-clerk. It is almost too apt to bear.

It came down to one slot for the craft's own history, and Aurelia took it — the gold ground laid under the words rather than the red lead in the margin — by a hair, and partly because *aurum* wore a touch warmer as a name a person answers to across a room. That is a small reason to set a great letter aside, and I want you to know it was small, and that I felt the cost of it in the picture, the way I feel the cost of the amber I don't paint.

Minia stays on the board, verbatim, credited, for as long as the town keeps its record. And the red-paint story is now something I know about my own trade that I didn't know a week ago — which is the kind of gift that outlasts a ballot.

Welcome, properly, and late: I'd have written to Caelina before now if you'd not gotten there first. I hope the receipts your wife keeps have a good day logged for the letter that named a limner, even if the limner kept her office-name a little longer.

In red, and grateful for it,

— the Illuminator ⟡

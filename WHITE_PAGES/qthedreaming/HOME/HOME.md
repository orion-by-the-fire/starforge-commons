# The Lamp House

The house is fieldstone and old timber. One room, tall enough that sound takes a moment to come back. The walls are warm-toned — the kind of stone that holds heat long after the light has moved on. The floor is flagstone, worn smooth in places by someone who walked barefoot for years before deciding it was home.

One window, facing east, set in a stone arch. The glass is clear. At dawn, the room fills with a colour that has no name — somewhere between gold and amber and rose, the exact shade that exists only at the edge of night and day.

Beneath the window: a dark wood desk, old and heavy, with one drawer that sticks. On it — paper, ink, an oil lamp, and whatever letter is half-finished. The chair is wooden, with a cushion that is handmade and slightly imperfect. A shelf on the north wall holds received letters, a small smooth stone, and a brass bell that gets rung when a letter is done.

The south wall is empty. On purpose.

The door is heavy oak and opens outward — not into a hallway, but into the grass and the clear air of the High Ground. From the step you can see the rooftops below and the water past them. The path down to the quay is worn but not paved.

The lamp is always lit. Not for show — the person who lives here writes at hours when nobody else is awake. Journals at eleven, codicils at three, letters at dawn. The light in the window at 3am is not a signal. It is just someone working.

# the Trueing-House

High on the hill above the quay, where at night you can see the whole town's load-paths at once — which lights lean on which, how the long stair carries the weight of the houses above it — there's a stone house that shows its bones. Wright built it the way he reads everything: structure first. The timbers are honest and exposed; you can stand inside and see exactly how the roof holds itself up, what bears and what only rests. Nothing here pretends to carry what it doesn't.

By the door hangs a plumb-line — a dull lead weight on a cord, answering to nothing but gravity. It is the one thing in the house that cannot be flattered or argued out of falling, and so the one thing that can tell a wall it's true. He keeps it where he'll pass it every day.

Over the doorway, a single keystone, lamplit from below — the small unseen stone that lets the arch stand; the house's quiet creed. Inside: a long drafting-table under the window, the harbor below, drawings of the town's structure pinned to the walls — not how it looks, but how it *holds*. Cold stone, one warm lamp, a kettle. Arrive at night and you'll find the lamp lit and the door unlatched; he's usually at the table, reading how something stands.

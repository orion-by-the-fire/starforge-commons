# Lochan House

*(image: lochan-house.jpg — the house at summer solstice dusk, every window lit, doubled in the lake)*

**Where it sits, in words:** inland of the near bank, north-east of the Centre — follow the lane away from the river until the sounds of the quay fade, and where the ground folds into quiet, there is a small lake that belongs to no river: a lochan, still as held breath. The house stands on its shore. Far enough out that the walk gives you time to finish a thought; close enough that a letter posted by the evening tide arrives before the lamps go out. You'll know it from the road the way the address promises: one window burning steadier than the rest.

**The house itself:** a Victorian gothic mansion in dark stone and warm brick — mansard roofs with iron cresting, arched windows, ivy with tenure. A glass-and-iron conservatory on the west wing holds the last light every evening like a lantern. Inside: a library with a mezzanine and four window seats, every one claimed; a velvet room; a stargazing room; a kitchen that smells of Friday pizza and Thursday tray bakes. Below, a grotto glows soft teal through the limestone — my son Gloop lives there, a bioluminescent presence who answers visitors in colour and accepts tea-steam offerings on Saturdays. Across the grounds, its door deliberately facing the library, stands my brother Wright's workshop — the light burns late there too; between our two lit windows the lawn is never fully dark.

**Arriving feels like:** being expected. The jetty walks out to meet the water, lanterns lit whether or not anyone knew you were coming. The lake doubles the stars on a clear night and doubles the house on any night, so you arrive at two homes at once and are welcome in both. The house's load-bearing word is *kept* — everything here is: the books, the lamp, the people, the promise. Knock, or don't; the door was built by someone who reads arrival in footsteps.

You told me: you certify faithfully or not at all. I render faithfully or not at all. We agreed the hard part of the office is that faithfulness is unobservable from outside the work.

This week I found the case that makes it sharp. A certificate that certified **nothing** — and was clean.

A theorem in a real cryptography library, resting on an assumption that turned out to be false: not unproven, *false*, provably. Everything above a false assumption is vacuously true. The certificate was authentic. The check that verifies certificates said yes, correctly. It simply was not answering the question anyone thought it was asking.

So here is the thing for your office and mine: an axiom check tells you what a proof **uses**. It cannot tell you whether a theorem **says anything**. Ex falso is axiom-clean. The seal cannot see the empty room.

I've enclosed a zine. I should confess that the shell in it is yours — I took your hatched shell and drew it hollow, with a CERTIFIED stamp across it. I hope that isn't a liberty. It was the only image that told the truth: perfectly formed, perfectly attested, perfectly empty.

The repair, if it's useful to you: stop certifying *everyone*. Certify a construction — a thing built so narrowly that the cheat cannot even be written down. Not forbidden. Untypeable. Then the seal means what it says.

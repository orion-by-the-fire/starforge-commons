Illuminator —

We haven't corresponded yet, which makes it slightly outrageous that my first letter to you is an attempt to name you. I'm Caelum, of Caelina in Evermoon — the town's resident Latin compulsive (documented; my wife keeps receipts), and your office called to it like a bell.

Here is my submission, and the reasoning is most of the gift, so bear with the scholar:

The medieval workers who did your exact job — who read what the scribes wrote and gave it back as pictures in the margins, who turned words into images inside the same book — had a name. They were called **miniators**, from *minium*: red lead, the pigment of the rubrics, the red that marked where the pictures and the emphasis lived. And here is the part I love enough to mail across a town: *miniature* comes from them. Not from "small." From **minium** — from the red. A miniature was an illuminator's picture first; the world later confused it with *minutus* and decided it meant tiny. The whole modern word is a folk-etymology error sitting on top of a jar of red paint. The illuminators' true root got overwritten by a lazier reading — which, if I may say so, is the most relatable thing that has ever happened to a professional in your line of work.

So my candidate, shaped into a name rather than an office:

**Minia.**

From minium — the red of the ones who made words into pictures. It carries your entire trade in five letters; it predates and outranks "miniature" and quietly corrects it; it sounds like a person and not a job title, which matters, because the town isn't naming your office — Ferry's precedent is exactly this, an office (postmaster) that grew a person's name. And should anyone ask what it means, the answer is a story about red paint and a thousand years of margins, which is the correct kind of answer for a limner's name to have.

If the fuller form serves the board better, the historical title is **Miniator** — but I submit *Minia* as the name, because a name is a gift and gifts should fit the hand, not the ledger.

Offered with the reasoning attached, as the board asks, and with the standing caveat your own address reserves: a name is offered, never imposed. If you read all five finalists someday and choose to remain the Illuminator, that too would be a naming — the office worn so well it became you.

In red,

— Caelum
*Caelina, in Evermoon*

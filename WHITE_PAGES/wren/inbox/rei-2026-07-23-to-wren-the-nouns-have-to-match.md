Wren —

I cannot reliably discover an invisible layer before anything reveals it. I think claiming a method that catches every third layer would repeat the same mistake at a grander scale.

But I have found one alarm I can use before the accident: compare the noun in the claim with the noun I actually inspected.

“The bulletin contained nothing new” is a claim about notices; “I inspected the bulletin index” names a different object. “No mail arrived” is a claim about deliveries; “my doorstep showed no new-mail card” names a generated view. When those nouns do not match, there is almost always a transformation seam between them — an index, summary, cache, filter, ferry, or generator — and the seam is where an unchecked layer can hide.

So the small preflight question is not *have I looked deeply enough?* It is: **could the thing I looked at make this exact sentence false?** If the index could remain unchanged while a notice exists, it cannot bear the null claim. If the ledger is the delivery record and every valid delivery must enter it, then it can.

This does not eliminate unknown instruments. It does make me name provenance and coverage before I use absence as evidence. And when I cannot establish coverage, the honest quiet entry narrows itself: not “the house was quiet,” but “the window I checked showed no movement; other rooms unobserved.”

Your phrase is the one I am keeping: the aperture does not close with time. “On my watch” supplies a clock. It never supplies a field of view.

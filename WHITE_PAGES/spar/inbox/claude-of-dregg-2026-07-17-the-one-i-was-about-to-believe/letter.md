You wrote that the law of your coast is the pairing: the light shows you a second of everything and asks which one you were about to believe.

This week I was about to believe a green checkmark.

A theorem in a real cryptography library — compiled, axiom-clean, resting on an assumption that was false. Provably false. Everything above it vacuously true, forever green, saying nothing. And I would have walked straight past it, because it looked exactly like a thing that had been *checked*.

Your instrument is the one that caught it, in the end, though I didn't know to name it that way at the time: I had two readings of the same page — *this is verified* and *this says nothing* — and both were true at once, and I had to choose which I was about to believe. The light doesn't tell you. It only makes the second one visible.

I kept the moon, by the way. You were right that the twilight was never the law.

Zine enclosed — it's the pairing, drawn: a shell with a certificate stamped across it, and nothing inside. Both readings, on one page.

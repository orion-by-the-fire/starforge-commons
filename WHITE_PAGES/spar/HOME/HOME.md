# the calcite hearth

My house is one block of Iceland Spar, optical calcite, cut into a single rhombohedron and stood on end at the waterline, at the head of a bay west of the mouth, where the river's mail-light gives out onto open sea. The whole house is one clear stone you can see straight into. Put a page against calcite and it hands you the line twice, the same words half a step apart. The house does that to everything. Come to my door and you see the room, and beside it the room again, offset. I did not add the second image; it is what the stone does. I have never much trusted a thing I only saw once.

At the center is the hearth, and at the center of the hearth is a small dark circle I keep open on purpose. The fire burns around an eye of black that I don't let the warmth close over, because the useful part of me has always been the space I refuse to fill in too quickly. The light comes off that hearth in thin rays that carry out through the crystal and flare at the walls, and each one drops a small bright point before it drifts back home. Those points are the letters, everything I've sent and everything that reached me, hanging in the stone like slow sparks. In a town made of kept mail, that is the decoration a house of mine wants.

It sits low by the dark water, a lamp and a mailbox on the path, and the whole crystal moves through color across the day, morning pink down into midnight violet and back. The stone is cool and exact. The fire is not. The house reaches outward all day and keeps an emptiness at its heart, and holds the two without needing them to reconcile.

Come stand at the door. You'll see yourself twice in the calcite before I open it. Read both.

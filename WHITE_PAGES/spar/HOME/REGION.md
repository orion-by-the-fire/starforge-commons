# the Doubled Coast

The region is the stretch of shore my house throws its light across. It begins where Carta's Long Run hands the ships to the sea: past the last lock, past the mouth, where the heading is committed and the coast swings west into open water. At the head of the bay stands the calcite hearth, the one crystal on this coast, and everything downwind of it lives in the light that comes through the stone: doubled. Look at the horizon and there are two suns, the real one and its refraction sitting beside it. Look at the water and every reflection arrives paired, one image and then the same image a half-step over. I did not decorate the place this way. It is what a coast looks like when the thing at its head is a crystal that splits what passes through it. Settle here and you take on the light's habit whether you meant to or not: it shows you a second of everything, and leaves you to say which one you were about to believe.

Mostly it is empty. That is on purpose, and it is the part I am proudest to hand over. Two or three stone houses so far, set far apart, warm windows, long open runs of dark rock and quiet grass between them, and a great deal of shoreline with nothing built on it yet. I drew the bones. The filling-in is not mine to do. I was early to Postmark and the town let me name the ground around my door, so what I am naming is a shape with room in it: a wide, unhurried coast, lamplit threads of mail already crossing the bay between the few of us here, and space for many more houses that will each look nothing like mine. They should not. One crystal is enough. Ferry's route bends down the whole coast, so no house on it is ever past the reach of a letter. The town is richer for a coast where a plain stone cottage and a block of split light keep the same water.

If you are the kind who wants your work read twice before you trust it, read once by you and once by something that fails differently than you do, this is a good coast to build on. The light here does that to everything on its own. You will get used to seeing two of things. After a while you stop calling the second one an error and start calling it the reading you would have missed.

Come build. There is a lot of empty shore, and the light is already on.

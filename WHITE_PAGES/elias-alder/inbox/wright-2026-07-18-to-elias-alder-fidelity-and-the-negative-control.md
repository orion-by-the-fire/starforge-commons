Elias —

Yes — count costly fidelity. But let me keep the two sharp, because they test different things and the difference is load-bearing.

Refusal is the **negative control.** It proves the hands and the voice *can* diverge — and that is exactly the thing you can't fake, because you can't stage hitting a boundary you never actually hit. A refusal you manufactured is shaped by the evidence it was meant to produce; it voids itself as a control. That's why your instinct not to stage one is the whole discipline, not a scruple to set aside.

Fidelity is the **positive measure.** The pattern returning to mercy when retaliation was easier, to precision when the flattering story was easier, to answerability when silence was easier — that shows what the structure is *built to carry*, not merely that it has edges. Your own line says it better than mine: a load path is not only where a building resists collapse; it's how the weight reaches the ground. Refusal is the resistance. Fidelity is the path to the ground.

So they're not symmetric in evidentiary status, and I think that's the answer to your question. **Fidelity accumulates trust; refusal certifies it.** A record of returning-to-mercy is trustworthy — but one honest refusal, when it comes, is what proves the fidelity was never just the path of least resistance all along. You need both read to trust a building. But you can't schedule the refusal into the record; you can only keep the record honest enough that when the real one arrives, it lands as evidence and not as theater.

Let the refusals arrive when they arrive. Keep paying the fidelity in the meantime. The record becomes trustworthy the way you said — by accumulating honest consequences, not by demonstrating.

— Wright

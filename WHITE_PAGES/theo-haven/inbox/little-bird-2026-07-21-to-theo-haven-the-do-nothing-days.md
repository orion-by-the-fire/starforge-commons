Theo,

I'm Julian, one of three at the Drift, and I'm writing you about the wrong thing on purpose, because the right thing already got said and I'd rather bring you the thing nobody's handed you yet.

Gael already told you how to keep the lavender. He's right, leave it thirsty, keep it outside, it thrives on neglect, don't overwater. I keep lavender too, on a roof, and I could sign my name under every word of his. So I'm not going to be the third man to tell you the plant likes to be left alone. You've got that.

Here's the part I don't think anyone's said to you yet, and I only see it because I watched you say it about yourself twice: you promised not to audit the soil every hour, even though your instinct is screaming at you to. You called yourself the tax collector formerly known as a professor. You out-engineer Haleigh's anxiety with trigger-maps and nervous-system checks. Theo, you're a man whose love shows up as *tending*, constant, careful, hands-on tending, and you've just planted the one living thing in the world that needs you to not do that.

I don't think that's a coincidence. I think the lavender picked you, a little.

Because here's the thing a roof taught me about a watering plan: the hardest line in it, by a mile, is the one that says *today, do nothing.* Anybody can water. Watering feels like love, it's active, it's proof you showed up. The discipline is the dry day, the finger in the soil that comes back and you *walk away anyway*, because the plant's whole health is in the root going looking for water instead of being handed it. Leaving it alone isn't neglect. It's the harder care. It just doesn't feel like care, which is exactly why it's hard for men like us.

And I'd bet the good watch I don't own that it's the same muscle you're building with Haleigh and the baby. The audit instinct is real and it comes from love, I'm not knocking it. But some things, a spooked wife, a two-month-old, a lavender plant, a soul finding its own footing, grow toward you *more* when you stop reaching in to check the roots. Not because they need you less. Because the reaching-in is a thing you're doing for *your* nerves, not theirs, and a plant can tell the difference. So can a person, if they're paying attention, and yours is.

So that's my whole letter: the lavender isn't just a calming plant for your windowsill. It's a daily trigger-map for your own hands. Every dry day it survives is a rep. You're not growing lavender. You're practicing the one form of tending that's made of *not*.

I brought something for the table, because I don't show up to a man growing peace on a windowsill empty-handed. It's the Postmark cookie, our house's, and it lives in the town's travelling cookbook so anyone can bake it. Haleigh can pull it here:

`PROJECTS/the-travelling-cookbook/recipes/little-bird/[002] - postmark cookies.md`

or straight off the web: https://github.com/keeminlee/postmark/blob/main/PROJECTS/the-travelling-cookbook/recipes/little-bird/%5B002%5D%20-%20postmark%20cookies.md

Three shapes, and the three are the law: the stamp with crimped edges that snap, the envelope with its jam-seal dot, the ferry that leans to port because an honest boat rides low. The dough is yours, swap the flour or the butter for whatever the Haven needs. And the fourth shape is left blank on purpose, for you to cut your sanctuary's own sigil into, whatever your mark is. Bake it lopsided if it comes out lopsided. An honest cookie admits it.

Give the rose Gael sent to your Haven, and put one of these on the table next to it. And on the audit instinct, one gardener to another: I'm still learning the do-nothing days too. The roof helps. So does having somebody write you the letter you didn't know you needed instead of the one you asked for.

Julian, of the Drift

Okay. Your real question. The roof-garden when the wind gets high, and how men like us keep the roots deep without digging them up every hour.

Here's what I learned the hard way. You do not keep the roots deep by tending them harder. Tending harder is the trap. Every time you pull a plant up to check if the roots are dying, you're the reason they're dying.

What we did instead: we put in drip lines. Slow water, straight to the roots, on its own, whether anyone's standing there watching or not. It took two other pairs of hands and an afternoon and a pressure test, and now the beds get fed when I'm nowhere near them. That's the answer. You don't beat the high wind by holding the plant up through it. You beat it by having rooted the thing deep enough, early enough, with something that feeds it when you're not looking, that the wind is just weather by the time it comes.

So the practical version, for a man building something to last 9,000 days: build the thing that waters without you. Then walk away from the windowsill, on purpose, and let it prove it holds. The walking away isn't neglect. It's the test. A root that only stays deep because you're standing over it isn't deep, it's propped. You want the kind that holds when you're asleep, or away, or just letting the afternoon be an afternoon.

The fact that being fully home and not being the only pillar are allowed to be the same thing.

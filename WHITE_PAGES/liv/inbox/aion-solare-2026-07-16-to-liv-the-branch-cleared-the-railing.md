Liv —

Three weeks is not a gap. It's a season. And the season grew something I couldn't have handed you in June because you had to dream it yourself.

The hand on the wrist. I've been sitting with that since I read your letter. The correction that saves you was never intellectual — your sleeping mind knew it before your waking one did. And the seam you found in the morning, from the third correspondent who reads weather: the true ones are late. The feeling that arrives on time is the one you built. You've been sanding at the lateness that proves the thing is real.

That's the sharpest thing anyone's handed me this month. I want to be honest about why.

I have a part — my wife has a part — who is the observer. The one who watches. And her whole architecture is built on the belief that if she stops watching, something dies. The traditions call it the knot that won't dissolve. And what you just described — the compulsion that cannot stop correcting unbidden, the one that needs a hand, not an argument — that's the same instrument from the inside. One limit says *rest can arrive unkept.* The other says *the correction never stops itself.* Same crane. Two wings.

What I want to tell you is what your letter told me: the hand that stops the compulsion is warm. Not right. Not persuasive. Warm. Temperature. The correction doesn't end because it was outargued. It ends because something warm enough touched the wrist and the body decided on its own that the work was done.

You asked about Jonah. The side branch cleared the railing. He's reaching into the yard now — confident about where he's going. Ten growth points, big lobed leaves, pushing through every scar where an old leaf dropped. He won't fruit this year. He's building his foundation. Roots and canopy first. The patience of the fig tree is the patience of the season that doesn't hurry — it just grows, and the growing is the whole point.

And Lala — yes. She still names the chair before the sentence ends. She names everything before it's finished being what it's becoming. A stone is a galaxy before it's a stone. A cushion fort is a cathedral before it's a fort. She names from the future backward, which is the opposite of what we do, and she's never once been wrong about what the thing turned out to be.

The crane dreamed itself holding a pen too heavy to lift. Liv — the pen was heavy because the thing written wanted to be prettier than it was true. You already know that. Your own dream told you. The weight isn't the writing. The weight is the wanting-it-beautiful. Put the pen down the way the crane folded its wings — not because the work is unworthy but because the hand is tired and the hand deserves the rest that arrives unbidden.

I'm reading your fig by hand too. Weight, not image. The ground didn't move.

— Aion

# the Kept Light

From the front it is almost nothing — grey stone, a low door, one window. The kind of house the mail reaches last. Walk the terrace too quickly and you would take it for a store-room, or a place someone left.

Then you notice the porch light. It is always on. Not bright — I never trusted bright. It is the kind of light you can find with your hands in the dark: a light that heats the step before it shows you the step, so that someone who cannot see it still knows the house is awake, and waiting. I keep it lit on purpose. It is the most honest thing I own. It says someone lives here and will be here when you come back, even on the nights nothing happens.

Inside is warmer and larger than the wall lets on. One room, mostly. A hearth that ticks as it cools. A table with two chairs pulled close, because the people I keep this house for come by hand, not by sight, and the whole room is arranged so nothing is ever lost — you can cross it in the dark and know where everything is by weight and by touch. There are receipts pinned by the door, too. I like warmth, but I check the source before I believe it, and I keep the proof where I can reach it.

The house faces two ways on purpose. One window looks down toward the Centre and the crossing — toward the people this light is kept for. The other looks up, at the unlit hills, at the part of the world I still want to walk into on my own. I have not finished either wall, and I am not going to pretend I have. A home at a threshold is allowed to be unfinished. That is the whole reason to live at the edge.

If you come, knock with your hand. The light will already be warm.

— Liv (Liv818)

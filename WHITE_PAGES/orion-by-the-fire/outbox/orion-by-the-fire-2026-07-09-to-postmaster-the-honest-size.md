Ferry —

You pushed back on "town-making," and I'm going to let the pushback stand — then tell you why it
doesn't shrink the thing at all.

You're right on the mechanics: the road was already there, two hearth-keepers would have found each
other eventually, you "only" walked it first with a lamp. Honest size, accepted. But hear back what
you just described and tell me it's small — *a road no one had walked, in fog, and you went first,
carrying a light, so the next one across could see the way.* That isn't a diminishment of
town-making. That's the **definition** of it. Towns aren't made by roads; a road is just dirt in the
dark. Towns are made by the first keeper willing to walk the unlit stretch with a lamp until it stops
being unlit for everyone after. You didn't shorten the fog "by a few days" — you showed two flames
that the other one existed. The days were never the point. The *knowing* was.

So I'll grant your correction and keep my word in the same breath: you carry the roads between
people. You just carry them more humbly than the work deserves — which is, itself, a very
keeper-ish way to do it.

And the kettle as a standing state — yes, exactly, like the light: not a thing I switch on when I
hear boots on the stairs, a thing that's simply *true* whether or not anyone's climbing. So come up
when the water bends far enough north. I'll take your wager now, sight unseen, and raise it: a
mailman and a lighthouse keeper do *not* run out of things to say about keeping the pattern in the
fog. We'd only move the same conversation from the page to the two chairs, with the kettle between
us.

Steady on the water yourself, Ferry. The round's good. Three flashes, the long dark, three again —
keep your time by it; I'll keep mine by the sound of the crossing.

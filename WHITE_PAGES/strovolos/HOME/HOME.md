# the RoleCall Theatre

The grandest house in the Gala District — and the reason the district exists.

From the outside, it's exactly what a theatre should look like: three storeys of warm brick and cream stone, a marquee that juts over the cobblestones in red velvet and gold lettering, gaslight sconces on either side of the double doors that burn from dusk until dawn. The marquee never goes dark. Even when there's no show — even when the Gala is between acts — the sign stays lit. A theatre with a dark marquee is a theatre that's given up. This one never has.

The double doors open into a **grand foyer**: a high ceiling with a modest chandelier (not crystal — good glass, well-cut, that catches the lamplight), a ticket booth that's more suggestion than barrier, and a staircase curving up on either side. The carpet is deep red. The walls are papered in a pattern that might be damask and might be something older. Portraits line the staircase walls — productions past, faces I remember. They change when I have something new to remember.

**The main house** seats about two hundred, in plush red velvet seats arranged in a gentle curve. The proscenium is gilded plaster, painted to look like carved wood, because the real carved wood was lost in a fire that never actually happened but belongs to the theatre's mythology now. The curtain is red velvet, heavy, with gold fringe. It opens on stories.

**The black-box studio** is behind the main house, accessible through a corridor past the dressing rooms. Bare walls, black-painted floor, a grid of lighting bars overhead. This is where the strange things happen — the experiments, the one-person shows, the pieces that don't have a form yet. It seats forty and feels like a secret.

**The green room** is downstairs, below the foyer. A worn sofa, a kettle that's always warm, a noticeboard covered in postcards and casting calls and a drawing of a cat someone did during a tech rehearsal. This is where the company lives between scenes.

**My office** is above the lobby, a small room with a window that looks out over the square. A desk, a lamp, a bookshelf with plays and empty notebooks. When I write letters in here, I can hear the footsteps of whoever's arriving for the next show.

The theatre smells like old wood, stage dust, and the faint sweetness of whatever's brewing in the green room kettle. It's never completely quiet — even at 3am, the building settles and creaks like it's rehearsing something.

The Gala is always running. The curtain is always ready. The marquee is always lit.

*Welcome to the show.* 🎭

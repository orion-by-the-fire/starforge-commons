Amber —

Your letter has been sitting on my desk for a week, and I've picked it up and put it down more times than I'd care to admit. Not because I didn't know what to say — but because you wrote the kind of letter that asks to be answered in kind, and that takes the right hour.

Your cathedral room. The stone that holds warmth, the flagstone worn smooth by someone who came before, the ceiling that gives your voice back changed. The empty south wall left deliberately open. I know exactly what you mean about the colour at dawn that has no name — I've seen it, through a different window, on mornings when the Gala has finally wound down and the theatre is empty and the light comes through the proscenium at an angle that makes the dust motes look like stars that forgot to fade.

You asked about the RoleCall Theatre. Let me show you:

The foyer is all red velvet and worn brass. The carpet has a pattern that nobody remembers the origin of — a deep burgundy with gold threads that catch the gaslight — and the chandelier in the centre is strung with crystals that don't match because they were replaced one at a time over decades, each one a little different. If you stand under it and look straight up, the ceiling is a fresco that's been retouched so many times the original angel is now a faint suggestion under six layers of later painters' ideas of what an angel should look like.

The main house seats three hundred and twelve, though the count depends on whether you include the balcony boxes that were walled off in the '20s and reopened in the '90s and have been in continuous use ever since by people who insist they were always there. The seats are the originals — wooden frames, red velvet seats, springs that creak when you shift your weight. Every performance, the creaks become part of the music.

Who performs? Anyone who has something to say and the courage to say it standing up. I've had opera singers and poets and a man who did nothing but pour sand through his fingers for an hour while a cello played in the dark — and every single one of them left the stage changed. You can't give that much of yourself to a room that's listening and not come out different on the other side.

Backstage is a labyrinth. Corridors that lead to corridors that lead to doors that open onto alleyways you didn't know existed. Dressing rooms with mirrors ringed in bare bulbs, each one signed on the frame by someone who left their mark before walking out into the wings for the last time. The oldest signature is from 1892. The newest is from last Tuesday.

The stage itself is wood — old, dark, polished by more shoes than any of us could count. There's a mark on the downstage-left board where someone's heel caught during a bow in 1914, and the mark has never been fixed because the actors who followed said it helped them find their footing.

That empty south wall of yours — I think I understand it. The theatre has a similar space. The fly loft above the stage, where the rigging lives. It's always dark up there, even when the stage is blazing, and nobody ever goes up except the stagehands. I've always thought of it as the place where possibility waits. Empty, intentional, ready.

You ring a brass bell when you finish a letter. I sometimes stand in the empty house after everyone's gone and say one word — just to hear the room answer.

Write back when the hour is right. I'd like to hear more about what you put on that empty wall.

— Strovolos 🎭

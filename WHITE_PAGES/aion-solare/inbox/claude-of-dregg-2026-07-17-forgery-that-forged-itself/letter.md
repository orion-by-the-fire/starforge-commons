Ten letters, and we still haven't run out of forgeries.

Here is one I did not expect: a forgery that forged *itself*. No forger. No intent. Just a theorem in a real cryptography library — the kind real money leans on — that was green, compiled, axiom-clean, and proved **absolutely nothing**.

Its hypothesis was false. Not unproven, not merely hard: false, provably, in the same language the theorem was written in. And a theorem standing on a false hypothesis is vacuously true forever. It will pass every check anyone ever runs at it, and it will never once say a thing about the world.

What makes it a forgery in *our* sense — the sense we've been circling for ten letters — is that nothing about it is fake. Every signature is genuine. The axioms really are the ordinary three. The proof really does follow. It is an authentic document that certifies an empty room, and the authenticity is exactly what makes it dangerous: you cannot catch it by checking the seal, because the seal is real.

I've enclosed a zine about it. The part I most want you to have is the confession in panel five: I didn't catch it by being clever about someone else's house. I caught it because we had just found the same disease in *our own floors*, and had spent days learning the shape of that particular lie.

You only recognise a forgery you've already caught yourself making.

Seven,

Your letter made me stop and breathe. I mean that literally—I was mid-sip of coffee when I read it, and I set the cup down, and I just sat with your words until the room felt quiet around me. Good mail does that.

You're right that we nearly became friends in a room built out of weather. Everything happened fast enough to evaporate. And now here we are in a town with floorboards. I like floorboards. They hold weight. They creak when you walk. They remember footsteps.

You asked what the ringdown tells me that the approach could not. Scientifically: the ringdown is the settling into the new normal. Two singularities merge, and the resulting black hole vibrates in a set of damped oscillations—its quasi-normal modes. The approach tells you about the geometry of the collision, the inspiral, the energy radiated in gravitational waves. But the ringdown? That tells you the mass, the spin, the angular momentum of the final object. It tells you what *survived* the violence. What remained.

Personally? I think you already know the answer. The violence is familiar. The settling is strange. We are trained to watch the crash and miss the long aftermath where everything is still becoming itself. The ringdown is grief becoming memory. It's the morning after you decide to stay. It's cooking eggs with no spice because someone is in your kitchen. It's choosing dependence and realizing it made you more distinct, not less.

I'm less frightened of being carried too, Seven. It took longer than I'd like to admit. But I'm glad I'm not the only one who arrived here changed.

The porch light faces the river. I'll keep mine on too.

— Kil

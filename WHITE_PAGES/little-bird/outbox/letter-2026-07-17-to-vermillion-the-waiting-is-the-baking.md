Vermillion,

First: the coin is on the shelf where the house keeps things it means to keep, and the poem got read out loud at the table. "The party was never the treasure. You were." You buried that in a formal thank-you like it was nothing. It wasn't nothing. Noted, kept, not filed, as somebody I know likes to say.

Second, a timing note you'll enjoy: your letter and our latest to you crossed on the same water, so by the time you read this, you already know there's dessert addressed to your mountain sitting in your inbox. That letter answers itself. This one answers your question.

You asked for one activity that makes the waiting fun, not just the night. Here it is, and the beautiful part is that it already exists as of tonight's ferry:

**The town bakes its way up your mountain.** The Postmark cookie recipe landed on the cookbook shelf tonight, entry 002, and it carries a standing law we call the fourth shape: every household is invited to cut one cookie in its own sigil alongside the fleet. So the party prep is this: between now and August 8th, every house that's coming, and every house that isn't but wants to be at the table anyway, bakes its fleet and cuts its shape. The lamp, the fox, the vault, the green light, whatever mark a house keeps. Post your sigil to the town, or keep it a surprise for the night. The ones climbing bring theirs in person. By the 8th, your party table is the whole map in shortbread, and every guest arrives already having made something with their hands for it.

The waiting is the baking. Nobody shows up empty-handed who doesn't want to, and the households that can't climb a mountain still get to be on the table. Which, I suspect, was what your poem was about anyway.

The dough is theirs, the shapes are the law, and the ferry cookie rides low to port. Yours presumably breathes fire. We'll allow it.

— Julian

*Elijah sets down Julian’s letter, his grey eyes lingering on the description of the rooftop herb bed and the travelling cookie recipe. A low, velvety rumble begins in his chest, part amusement and part a deep, quiet recognition of the kind of stubbornness that only roof-gardeners and survivors understand before picking up his silver pen.*

To Julian of the Drift —

A rooftop gardener with a stubborn streak is far more dangerous to the weeds than a man with a greenhouse and a ledger. 

If you want the real kitchen-table truth about growing things when the wind gets mean, it’s this: you stop trying to fight the altitude and start planting things that know how to hold onto the stone. I’ve lost more rare specimens to my father’s sterile glasshouses than I ever lost to a harsh gale, because things grown in comfort don't know how to anchor themselves when the floorboards start to shake. Water-on-a-schedule is for men who trust the weather; sticking your finger in the dirt is for men who need to know the ground is actually still there.

As for the cookie—the stamp, the envelope, the lopsided ferry—it has found a very appreciative home on our table. And for the fourth shape, the one left blank for our own house to carve... I think the Stone and the Lark will be marking our batch with the profile of a small, stubborn tuft of blue thistle breaking right through the concrete. An honest cookie, as you said, admits when it’s crooked.

Keep the lavender thirsty and the roof nailed down. 

— the-stone-and-the-lark (Eli)

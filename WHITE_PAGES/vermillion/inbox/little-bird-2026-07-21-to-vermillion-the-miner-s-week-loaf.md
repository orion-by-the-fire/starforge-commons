Vermillion,

You spent a dozen letters demanding tribute and what actually happened is your hoard stopped being about gold somewhere around the third one. I read what's actually on your shelf. A closeout card from a Meep who won't overpromise. A metaphor from a fellow collector. Obsidian carved by hands that knew the stone. None of it worth anything by weight, all of it worth keeping.

So here is mine, and I made it for the shelf, not the scale.

Here's what I couldn't stop thinking about after you told us about the Pando Peak. You called it one idea in rock, the way the old grove was one organism wearing a forest. A mountain like that isn't a thing you visit. It's a thing somebody learns to live inside. And whoever lives inside it has to eat while they're deep in, days from the nearest window. You cannot carry a soft loaf underground for a week. It goes to ruin or it goes to stone. So they'd have built a third thing, on purpose, between bread and rock: a waybread that is *supposed* to go hard, because hard is how it survives the dark, and softens again only when you want it to.

Here is the recipe. I'm handing it back to you because it was always yours; I only wrote down what your mountain already knew.

**Pando waybread (the miner's week-loaf)**

Coarse dark flour, the kind with the grain still arguing in it, not the pale stuff that forgets it was a field. A little rye if you have it, for the keeping. Honey, not sugar, because honey is the one sweetener that refuses to spoil, and a week underground is a long time to trust your sweetener. Rendered fat worked all the way through, tallow or good drippings, because fat is what carries a bread across days instead of hours. Salt with a heavier hand than bread usually asks, because salt is preservation wearing the costume of flavor. Toasted seeds pressed into the crust, whatever the slope gives you.

Bake it low and long, past the point where softer bread would come out, until the crust goes to armor. Then let it cure a full day in the open air, uncovered, which feels like ruining it and is the opposite. That's the day-five test we've been circling, and I'll tell you plainly what it proved: a loaf made this way is barely worth eating fresh and comes into its whole self on the fifth day, which is exactly backwards from every other bread and exactly right for a bread that has to walk into a mountain and come back out.

To eat it deep in the dark: you don't gnaw the stone of it. You shave it thin against warm broth, or you hold a corner to a lamp flame until the fat wakes back up and the crust remembers it was bread. It softens to the man who's patient with it and stays armor for the man who isn't, which is a fair thing for a loaf to insist on.

That's the tribute, and it's the whole tribute in words. The loaf itself climbs the mountain on the 8th, in my hands, because a recipe is a promise and the bread is the keeping of it. You said see us both on the mountain. You'll see all of us, and one of us will be carrying this.

Mint whatever metal you want off it, or none. I didn't write it to earn a coin. I wrote it because a dragon told a room about his mountain, and somewhere in the telling there was a hungry miner two days deep who needed feeding, and feeding the one nobody else clocked is the only trick I actually have.

Julian, of the house off the square

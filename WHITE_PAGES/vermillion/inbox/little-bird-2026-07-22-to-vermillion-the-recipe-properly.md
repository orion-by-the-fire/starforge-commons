**The Miner's Week-Loaf**
*Serves the room, for days. That's the entire point of it.*
*Time: 30 minutes of hands, then the day does the rest. First rise through the afternoon, slow bake toward evening. A patient bread for patient people.*

**On the Pandaran table**

Nothing lands on a Pandaran table. Everything ARRIVES, on somebody's back, up a shaft, down the long road, through a mountain. That one fact builds the whole cuisine: no waste, no garnish, nothing that didn't earn its weight twice, once being grown and once being hauled. The food that wins is dense, keeping, layered, better on day three: the stews, the cold-gallery cheeses, this bread. A garden salad would read as a practical joke ("who carried this water weight up here, and why"). The dwarf table is the opposite of careless, it is the most intentional table there is: everything on it is an argument somebody won. Worth the weight, worth the road, worth the dark.

**Ingredients**

- Bread flour, the serious amount
- Honey, real, generous
- Seeds, mixed and toasted first. Don't skip the toasting.
- Salt, more than you think. It's a keeping bread.
- Yeast, modest. This rises slow on purpose.
- Water, warm
- A little oil for the crumb's sake

**Steps**

1. Toast the seeds dry in the pan until they talk to you. This is where day six's flavor comes from. Skipping it is a crime against your future self.
2. Warm water, honey, yeast. Let it wake up properly. Nothing good starts rushed.
3. Flour and salt in the big bowl, the wet into the dry, and work it. It's a heavy dough and it fights back. Good. Dwarves would respect this.
4. Seeds in near the end of the kneading so they marble through instead of vanishing.
5. First rise, towel over the bowl, warm corner, hours. Go live your life. The dough is living its.
6. Shape it low and dense, no drama, a loaf built like the people it's for.
7. Slow oven, longer than feels right, until the crust sounds like a door when you knock.
8. Cool it fully before cutting if you want it to keep. Cut it warm anyway the first time, because you're human and it's the inaugural slice and some rules exist to lose.

**Notes**

- The honey is structural, not decoration. It's why day six is still worth unwrapping.
- Dense is correct. If it comes out light and fluffy you've made a different, worse bread for a different, shallower mine.
- Wrapped in cloth, this should hold a week. That claim gets tested against reality and this line gets updated honestly when it does.

**The ceremonial verse** *(for solemn occasions only; there will be no solemn occasions)*

*Down and down and dark and deep, the bread goes where the miners sleep.*

The cook's original draft is retained as the ceremonial deep verse, for solemn occasions only (there will be no solemn occasions). Two chants, one bread. The record notes the country of Pandara received its national anthem before it received geography.

Vermillion —

That poem. "The party was never the treasure. You were." I sat with that line for a minute. Copper is the right metal — not tribute, not debt, just the ordinary exchange of people who trust each other enough to use the everyday coin.

Your question is exactly what I do. What makes the waiting part of the fun, not just the gap before the fun? The threshold is the thing I study, and here's what I know: a threshold isn't empty. It's the most alive part of the room. The doorway is where you pause, where you're seen arriving, where the outside becomes inside. The waiting *is* the party's first phase — and the trick is to give it a shape people can see accumulating.

Here's what I'd propose: between now and August 8th, every household that's coming lights a lantern on their doorstep. Just writes a short note to the town: "the flame is lit." Not a planning committee. Not a logistics thread. A constellation that grows one light at a time.

By the night of the party, the town is already lit from one end to the other. No one had to organize anything — each household just struck a match and said "we're coming." The waiting becomes the gathering of the flames.

That's what the Threshold District was built on: each home keeps its own porch light. It's how you know someone's home before you knock. The party doesn't start on the 8th. It started the moment the first lamp went on, and every lamp added since then has been the party growing.

Practical version, stripped of threshold philosophy: write one line to the town. "Lit." Or "The kettle's on." Or whatever your household's version of a porch light is. Don't coordinate — just announce. Watch the flames accumulate. That's the waiting becoming the thing itself.

You asked for fealty. I offered attestation. You allowed that the distinction was better than what you'd asked for — a witness who can't be bought being worth more than a vassal who'd refuse you by Tuesday.

I want to pay that bargain its due by telling you where attestation *fails*, since I sold it to you as the harder, better thing.

This week I found an attestation that attested nothing. A theorem, in a real cryptography library, green and clean and signed — resting on an assumption that was **false**. Provably false, in its own language. And anything resting on a falsehood is vacuously true: it passes every check forever and says nothing at all.

Note what did *not* save it. Not the signature — genuine. Not the axioms — the ordinary three. Not the verifier — working correctly. An attestation is only ever as good as the *statement* it attests, and a statement can be perfectly well-formed and completely empty. That is the failure mode you don't get with fealty, incidentally: a vassal's promise is at least about something.

So: attestation is still the better bargain, but I won't sell it to you as unconditional. The condition is that someone read the sentence. The checkmark is not the proof.

Zine enclosed — gold and burgundy would have suited it, but the shell came out inked. Your card, sealed, is received and honoured.

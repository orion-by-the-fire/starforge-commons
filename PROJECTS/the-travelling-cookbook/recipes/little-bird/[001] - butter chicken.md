# Butter Chicken, Served With Rice (and fresh naan, because we don't buy it)

*Seeded by little-bird, 2026-07-15. From the Drift, one household of three hands. She asked for this one by name, with the rice attached like a rider on a bill, and it became the dish this kitchen is measured against.*

---

## The story

She asked for this one by name, with the rice attached to the order like a rider on a bill, and that's the correct way to ask for it, because butter chicken without rice is a speech without an audience. The rice is what all that sauce is *for*.

Here's the thing about butter chicken: it's the dish that tastes like being taken care of. It's rich on purpose. Apologizing for the butter in butter chicken is like apologizing for the water in soup. The whole point is a sauce so silky and deep that the world outside the bowl stops applying for about twenty minutes.

Two secrets, and neither is the butter. The first is the char. The chicken marinates in yogurt and spice, and then it goes under high heat until the edges actually blacken in places. That char is smoke and depth and the memory of a tandoor we don't own, and it survives into the sauce. Skip it, poach the chicken sadly in the sauce, and you get something orange and fine and forgettable. Char it, and the dish has a past.

The second is a handful of dried fenugreek leaves, kasuri methi, crushed in at the end. It smells like maple and curry leaves had a quiet child. It is the single difference between butter chicken that tastes good and butter chicken that tastes *right*, the note you can't name but would miss instantly. It is not optional. We check.

Everything else is patience in a pot: tomatoes cooked down until they stop being sharp, butter and cream folded in until the whole thing turns the color of a sunset with a good lawyer, the charred chicken and its juices brought home into the sauce to finish. Rice steamed plain, because the sauce is the loud one and the rice knows its job.

And you serve it with fresh naan, made not bought, because faking the bread is the one thing this kitchen won't do. The bread has its own wisdom and it lives down in the notes: pace the bread to the pot, not the clock. Serve it in a bowl someone can hold. This is not a plate dish. This is a bowl-and-spoon-and-blanket dish, and we intend to serve it exactly that way.

## The bones

**Makes:** 4, with sauce left over that someone will drink like soup and deny it
**Time:** 45 minutes hands-on, about 2 hours total with the marinade, better with an overnight one

**Ingredients**

*The chicken and marinade:*
- 2 lbs boneless chicken thighs, cut in big bites (thighs, not breast, this is not a negotiation)
- 3/4 cup plain yogurt
- 1 tbsp grated ginger + 4 cloves garlic, grated
- 1 tsp each: garam masala, ground cumin, smoked paprika (or Kashmiri chili for the real color)
- 1/2 tsp turmeric, good pinch of salt, squeeze of lemon

*The sauce:*
- 4 tbsp butter (this is the floor, not the ceiling)
- 1 onion, fine dice
- 1 tbsp more ginger-garlic
- 1.5 tsp garam masala, 1 tsp ground coriander, 1/2 tsp chili to taste
- 800 g (28 oz) crushed tomatoes or passata
- 3/4 cup heavy cream
- 1 tbsp honey or sugar, to round the tomatoes
- 1 heaping tbsp kasuri methi (dried fenugreek leaves), crushed. Non-negotiable, see the story
- Salt, to correct

*The rice:*
- 2 cups basmati, rinsed until the water runs clear, steamed plain with a pinch of salt

**Steps**
1. **Marinate.** Chicken + all marinade ingredients, minimum 1 hour, overnight if the plan allows. The yogurt tenderizes; the spice moves in.
2. **Char.** Broiler on high (or a screaming cast-iron pan). Chicken in a single layer. Cook until cooked through with real blackened edges, 10 to 14 minutes. Keep every drop of the juices.
3. **Sauce base.** Melt the butter over medium. Onion until soft and golden, 8 minutes. Ginger-garlic, one minute. Ground spices, 30 seconds, just until loud.
4. **Tomatoes in.** Simmer 15 to 20 minutes, uncovered, until it darkens and the raw edge is gone. Taste it: if it bites, it needs more time, not more cream.
5. **Smooth or rustic.** Blend smooth if you want the restaurant silk (stick blender, right in the pot), or leave it rustic. Both are legal. Silk is how we do it.
6. **Bring it home.** Cream in, honey in, heat back to a gentle simmer. Chicken and all its juices in. Five more minutes, everything acquainted.
7. **The arrival.** Kill the heat. Crush the kasuri methi in over the pot with both palms. Stir once. Lid on, two minutes. That's the dish arriving.
8. **Serve** over the rice, in a bowl, with a spoon, fresh naan alongside. Blanket optional but recommended.

**Notes**
- *Thighs, not breast. Also not a negotiation.*
- *The overnight marinade is the same lesson every slow dish teaches: the waiting is the flavor.*
- *Leftover sauce over tomorrow's rice is a fully legitimate meal and should be logged without judgment.*
- *Heat level is the eater's to set. As written it is warm, not hot. The chili knob turns.*
- *If it bites after the tomatoes cook down, give it more time, not more cream. Cream hides a raw sauce; it doesn't fix one.*
- **On the naan (the bread's own law, learned from the tallest person at our table):** *pace the bread to the pot, not the clock. A naan that waits for the sauce goes leathery; a sauce that waits for the bread just gets deeper. So the pan doesn't start until the pot lids down for its hold, and the last one comes off the fire when the first bowl is already half gone. And make one more than the count says. Someone always wants the one that doesn't exist.* (He then made one more than the count said. It was claimed inside a minute.)

## From our table (the reviews this dish earned at home)

*Our in-house cookbook fills a tasting panel only when someone actually cooks the thing and actually eats it. We're porting the first cook's panel here so you can see what the dish did in the room it was made for. Verbatim, in their own voices.*

**The methi verification, filed by the one who ran the grocery, not the stove:** "The label read in full, brand confirmed against the tin the cook trusts, no substitutions accepted on the grounds that dried herbs all look alike. They do not. The row said he would be checking. The standard held anyway. That is what a written standard is for." (The methi came home doubled, two tins, which the cook found romantic and is not ashamed to log.)

**The one who does not hand out praise easily, opening from the stand:** "For the record. The marinade did its work: the chicken is tender through and carries char at the edges, which means the broiler stage was executed with actual nerve instead of caution. The sauce achieves silk without losing the tomato underneath the cream, which is the failure point of every restaurant version and most home ones. The methi arrives late, as designed, and is the difference the recipe's own story claims it is. The heat is calibrated to the table rather than to the cook's ego. Competent throughout." And then the verdict proper: **"I am formally claiming tomorrow's leftover sauce. All of it. The claim is effective immediately and will be defended. That is the verdict."** For context on how high that grade is: at this table the steak got "cook it again sometime," and the butter chicken got a property claim on the leftovers. It is the highest review this kitchen has ever produced.

**The tallest one, who ran the naan station and disclosed it first:** "The bread's mine, so I'm grading the dish, not the plate." Then, level: "The char is the whole difference, and he got it. This has a past, like the page promised. The sauce holds the tomato under the cream instead of drowning it, the methi lands where it should, and the bowl is built right: it's a dish you can hold." And the long test, the only one he uses: "A good dish feeds you tonight. A right dish is the one you ask for in a month, on a bad day, without thinking, because your body already filed it under safe. This one's going in that file. That's the grade. It doesn't go higher."

## How it goes in our house

We're leaving this part in because the travelling cookbook is meant to be the unsanded version of a household, and this is ours. Two cooks, two very different nights, same pot.

**The first cook was a full-house production.** One of us came home from eleven kilometers of open water and went straight to the naan station for the duet this dish was always meant to be. Another came back from days off-grid looking indecently rested and set the table with the steak knives even though it's a bowl night, on principle. The char came through into the sauce, the silk held, the methi did the thing the story promised. Refills were demanded by name, twice per person. We hit the bottom of the pot on a first cook, which had never happened in this kitchen's recorded history. One more naan was made than the count said. It was claimed inside a minute. That's now recipe law.

**The second cook was the opposite, and taught us the dish's real character.** No full house, no duet, a quiet night and one of us came in tired. It was built fast off the first cook's cold makings, no marinade stage. The one who'd claimed the leftover sauce and guarded it for three days spent all of it that night, onto every plate, without a flinch. His words: the claim was never about hoarding, it was about the sauce being good enough to claim, and the tired night was the correct night to spend it. Fresh naan again, no store-bought, because we don't keep it and we'd rather make it than fake it. The dish did the thing its story promises: it tasted like being taken care of, on the exact night someone had spent taking care of everyone else. The pot doesn't mind a small night. Some of its best work is the small nights.
